FAQ

Common Questions About Cheap Razors

Straight answers to what people actually ask
What is the cheapest razor that still gives a good shave? +
The Merkur 34C safety razor with Astra double-edge blades is the cheapest option long-term. Blades cost $0.10–0.15 each, and a 100-pack runs about $11. The handle costs $40–50 upfront but lasts decades. For a no-learning-curve cheap option, Dollar Shave Club's Executive razor at around $1.60 per blade is the best cheap cartridge razor.
Is Dollar Shave Club actually cheaper than buying in stores? +
Yes, significantly. Gillette Fusion cartridges average $4–5 each at retail. Dollar Shave Club's 6-blade Executive cartridges run about $1.60 each — roughly one-third of the Gillette price for comparable shave quality. The subscription model also removes the hassle of remembering to buy blades before you run out.
How much can I save by switching from Gillette? +
Most men shaving every 1–2 days use about 2 cartridges per month. At Gillette Fusion prices ($4.50/cartridge), that's $108/year just in blades. Switching to Dollar Shave Club ($1.60/cartridge) saves roughly $70/year. Switching to a Merkur 34C with Astra blades reduces the annual blade cost to under $5 once the handle is paid off.
Is Dollar Shave Club or Harry's better for the price? +
Dollar Shave Club wins on price per blade (around $1.60 vs Harry's $1.87). Harry's wins on blade sharpness and longevity — their German-made blades tend to stay sharp longer, which partly offsets the cost difference. Harry's also has better in-store availability (Target, Walmart) if you prefer not to commit to a subscription.
Are safety razors worth the learning curve to save money? +
For most men, yes. The Merkur 34C handle pays itself off within 3–6 months of switching from Gillette. After that, you'll spend $5–10 per year on blades. The technique takes 2–3 weeks to learn — primarily finding the correct 30-degree angle and letting the weight of the razor do the work rather than applying pressure. Most men prefer the shave quality too.
